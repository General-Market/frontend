We built a database of 4,675 founders across 2,741 crypto projects and cross-referenced it with five years of CoinGecko market cap data. Every week from January 2021 to March 2026, we ranked tokens by market cap, matched them to their founders, and computed demographics for the Top 100, Top 500, and Top 2000.

The tiers use only validated tokens — "Top 100" means the 100 highest-market-cap tokens that we have confirmed founder data for, not the literal CoinGecko top 100. This ensures every data point represents a real, identified founding team.

All ages are adjusted per snapshot year. Someone listed as 40 in 2026 was 35 in 2021. This matters more than you'd think — without this correction, historical age data is systematically wrong.

## Average Founder Age

The average founder age in the Top 100 is 39.7. Top 500 is 39.3. Top 2000 is 38.8.

The gap between tiers is surprisingly small — less than one year separating the founders of the largest projects from the broader market. Crypto doesn't have a dramatic age divide between mega-caps and mid-caps the way traditional tech does.

Over the five-year period, average age has climbed by about 2.8 years across all tiers. Part of this is biological — founders age — but part reflects the maturation of the space. The projects that dominated in 2021 are still largely the same projects dominating in 2026. New entrants tend to be founded by people in their early 30s, but the top tier is increasingly occupied by veterans who built their projects during the 2017-2020 era.

| Tier | Avg Age | Age Change (2021-2026) | Founders |
|------|---------|----------------------|----------|
| Top 100 | 39.7 | +2.8 | 198 |
| Top 500 | 39.3 | +2.7 | 947 |
| Top 2000 | 38.8 | +2.8 | 2,143 |

The age distribution isn't normal. There's a heavy concentration in the 30-40 range and a surprisingly long tail above 50. Several Top 100 projects were founded by people in their 50s and 60s — mostly in infrastructure and DeFi protocols.

## Gender

93% male. Across all tiers. With almost no movement over five years.

This isn't a surprising finding, but the flatness of the trend is striking. Despite the broader tech industry's push toward diversity over this period, crypto founder demographics have barely moved. The Top 100 is 92.9% male. The Top 2000 is 93.1%. There's no meaningful difference between tiers.

When we look at team composition, the picture is even starker:

- **All-male teams** make up the vast majority across all tiers
- **Mixed-gender teams** (at least one male and one female co-founder) represent a small single-digit percentage
- **All-female teams** are statistically negligible — fewer than 0.5% in any tier

The mixed-gender percentage has shown slight growth since 2024, but it's from an extremely low base. At the current rate of change, reaching even 10% mixed-gender founding teams would take decades.

## Nationality

American founders dominate at roughly 25% of the Top 100. The next largest groups are Chinese, Russian, Canadian, and a cluster of European nationalities (British, German, French, Swiss).

The nationality mix shifts meaningfully across tiers:

- **Top 100:** American dominance is strongest here. The projects that achieve mega-cap status skew heavily toward US-based founders.
- **Top 500:** Chinese founders gain share. European representation increases.
- **Top 2000:** More globally distributed. Indian, Korean, and Southeast Asian founders become more visible.

Over time, the American share has been relatively stable. The biggest shift has been growing representation from Asian founders in the 500-2000 range, particularly from South Korea, Vietnam, and India.

### Team Nationality Composition

When we classify entire founding teams by nationality mix:

- **All US** — Teams where every founder is American or Canadian
- **All EU** — Teams where every founder is European
- **All CN** — Teams where every founder is Chinese/Taiwanese
- **Mixed** — Teams with at least one founder from a different region

About 15-20% of top projects have mixed-nationality founding teams. The rest are homogeneous — founders from the same country or region. This percentage has been slowly increasing since 2023, but single-nationality teams still dominate.

Projects with "Has US" nationality (mixed teams that include at least one American) tend to cluster in the upper tiers. Having an American co-founder correlates with higher market cap — though causality is unclear. It could be access to US capital markets, regulatory familiarity, or simply that the US has the largest pool of crypto-native builders.

## Education

Education data is the noisiest in our dataset — many founders don't publicly disclose their educational background, resulting in a large "Unknown" category. Among those with known education:

| Level | Description |
|-------|-------------|
| PhD | Doctoral degree — common in protocol/cryptography founders |
| Masters | MS, MBA, MEng — the most common known degree |
| Bachelor | BS, BA — standard undergraduate |
| University (unspecified) | Attended university but degree level unclear |

PhD holders are overrepresented in the Top 100 relative to the broader market. Protocol-layer founders — the people who designed consensus mechanisms, zero-knowledge systems, and novel cryptographic primitives — disproportionately hold advanced degrees. MIT, Stanford, and ETH Zurich appear most frequently among top university matches.

The education gap between tiers is modest. The Top 100 has slightly more PhDs and slightly fewer "Unknown" entries, which may simply reflect that higher-profile founders have better-documented backgrounds.

### Team Education Composition

- **All PhD teams** — Rare but concentrated in infrastructure protocols
- **Mixed Higher Ed** — Teams combining PhD, Masters, and Bachelor holders
- **Mixed with Unknown** — Most common category — at least one founder with known education and others without
- **No Education data** — Teams where no founder has documented education

The trend is toward more educational diversity within teams. Mixed education teams have been gradually increasing as crypto projects become more operationally complex, requiring a mix of technical depth (PhDs in cryptography) and business execution (MBAs, or no formal degree at all).

## University Representation

Among founders with identifiable university affiliations, the top schools are:

1. **MIT** — Consistently the most represented, particularly in DeFi and infrastructure
2. **Stanford** — Strong in both protocol design and application-layer projects
3. **Harvard** — More common in governance and institutional-facing projects
4. **UC Berkeley** — Particularly in cryptography and privacy-focused projects
5. **ETH Zurich** — Disproportionate presence in Ethereum-adjacent infrastructure
6. **Oxford / Cambridge** — Strong in UK-based DeFi protocols
7. **Tsinghua / Peking** — Dominant in Chinese-origin projects
8. **Carnegie Mellon** — Computer science-heavy, mostly in infrastructure
9. **UPenn / Wharton** — Business-oriented founders, often in DeFi or CeFi bridges
10. **NUS (Singapore)** — Growing presence, especially in Southeast Asian projects

The university share charts show these concentrations shifting over time as different sectors rise and fall in market cap. During DeFi summer, Stanford and MIT shares spiked. During the NFT boom, university representation became more diffuse.

## Age Diversity in Teams

We measured the age spread within each founding team:

| Spread | Meaning |
|--------|---------|
| 0-4 years | Same generation — founders within a few years of each other |
| 5-9 years | Moderate gap — perhaps a senior and junior co-founder |
| 10-19 years | Significant gap — different career stages |
| 20+ years | Multi-generational — rare but present in established projects |

The vast majority of teams have 0-4 year age spread — co-founders tend to be peers. Teams with 10+ year spreads make up roughly 5-10% of the top tiers and haven't changed much over time. Multi-generational teams (20+ years) are extremely rare, under 2%.

There's no strong correlation between age diversity and market cap tier. Same-age teams and diverse-age teams appear at similar rates across Top 100, Top 500, and Top 2000.

## When Do Different Age Groups Peak?

We tracked every token from its first CoinGecko listing (TGE) to its all-time high (ATH) and grouped by the founder's age at the time of TGE.

| Age at TGE | Median Days to ATH | Median ATH Multiplier | Sample Size |
|------------|-------------------|----------------------|-------------|
| 20-24 | 106 | 3.1x | 89 |
| 25-29 | 177 | 3.4x | 300 |
| 30-34 | 102 | 2.8x | 336 |
| 35-39 | 119 | 2.6x | 267 |
| 40-44 | 109 | 2.5x | 137 |
| 45-49 | 180 | 2.7x | 84 |
| 50-54 | 89 | 2.3x | 45 |
| 55+ | 17 | 2.3x | 45 |

Younger founders (20-24) reach ATH quickly — 106 days median. The 25-29 group takes longest at 177 days but achieves the highest multiplier at 3.4x. The 30-34 group is the fastest at 102 days with a solid 2.8x multiplier — the sweet spot of speed and magnitude.

The 55+ group reaches ATH in just 17 days with a 2.3x multiplier. This is likely survivorship bias — older founders who launch tokens tend to be well-known figures whose projects attract immediate attention and capital.

ATH multipliers decrease with age after the 25-29 peak. This doesn't mean younger founders are better. It likely reflects that younger founders take more risk — launching more speculative tokens that either moon or die. The survivors show high multipliers. The ones that went to zero aren't in the ATH data.

### Year-by-Year Breakdown

The TGE-to-ATH dynamics vary dramatically by launch year:

- **2017-2019 launches:** Long time to ATH (1,000-2,500 days for some tranches). These are ICO-era projects that took years to find product-market fit.
- **2020-2021 launches:** Shorter paths to ATH, often within months. DeFi summer and bull market dynamics compressed timelines.
- **2022 launches:** Very long TGE-to-ATH for the 20-24 age group (~750 days). Bear market launches by young founders took the longest to recover.
- **2023-2024 launches:** Moderate timelines across all age groups. More mature market conditions.
- **2025 launches:** Shortest across the board. New projects reaching ATH in weeks, driven by memecoin dynamics and faster market cycles.

## ATH and ATL Demographics

When we group tokens by the month their absolute ATH occurred and look at the founders behind them:

**Bull market ATH clusters (Q4 2021, Q4 2024)** show younger average founder ages and higher American representation. These periods produce the most ATH events — hundreds of tokens hitting all-time highs simultaneously.

**Bear market ATH clusters (2022-2023)** show fewer tokens hitting ATH, and those that do tend to have older founders with stronger educational backgrounds. Bear market outperformance correlates with technical depth.

**ATL patterns** (tokens hitting all-time lows) show roughly inverse demographics. Bear market ATL clusters are dominated by younger founders with less documented education. Bull market ATLs are rare and idiosyncratic.

## Methodology

**Data sources:**
- **Founders:** 2,741 companies and 4,675 founders with manually verified demographic data including age, nationality, gender, education, and university affiliation
- **Market data:** CoinGecko daily market cap snapshots from January 2021 to March 2026, sampled weekly
- **ATH/ATL:** Computed from maximum and minimum historical USD prices per token across the full CoinGecko dataset (since 2013 for some tokens)
- **TGE dates:** First appearance in CoinGecko data used as proxy for token generation event

**Matching:** 1,362 of 2,741 companies (49.7%) have CoinGecko IDs. Only matched tokens are included in all analysis. Unmatched companies are typically pre-token, defunct, or listed on exchanges not tracked by CoinGecko.

**Age adjustment:** All ages offset by the fractional year difference between the snapshot date and January 1, 2026 (the reference date for age data collection). This produces smooth age transitions rather than yearly steps.

**Limitations:**
- Gender data relies on public identification and may not reflect actual identity
- Education data has significant gaps (~40% unknown)
- Age data depends on public disclosure accuracy
- Survivorship bias affects ATH analysis (dead tokens still appear in ATL data but not in top tier rankings)
- "Founder" includes co-founders; a company with 3 co-founders contributes 3 data points to individual metrics but 1 data point to team composition metrics
